Montreal Diaries

Jan 31, 2024

It’s been almost 6 months since I moved to Montreal, and I wanted to share my thoughts on this journey. Moving out of a familiar territory was a mix of extreme excitement and fear. To give you some context, I had lived in the same house for over 10 years. I never experienced the difficulty of moving (I understand that it’s a privilege for which I am grateful), but I packed almost everything I needed into three suitcases and came to a new country with no permanent place to live, hoping to find a unique home.

The first day was mentally draining for many reasons. One incident that’s funny now, but wasn’t then, involved my eyes being glued to the Uber meter as I mentally converted each dollar. I had heard advice not to do this, but I’m still figuring out how to stop. Every small thing I did on my own felt like an achievement. I knew almost no one before coming here, but now I have a few friends I can depend on when things go sour. Home has taken on a different meaning (cliché, I know).

There are many things I see differently now. I learned to cook, manage a household, find a home during a housing crisis, lease a house to another tenant, balance classes and chores, draw boundaries, and open up to new people. Montreal is a beautiful city that I’m still exploring. You know those pieces of advice people give you, and inside, you think they’re wrong? Well, I got some severe reality checks that made me grow up and appreciate different things.

Remember those ideas we had in engineering college, like starting a Xerox shop? The abroad version is starting an Indian meal delivery service for students, making and selling dosa batter, or bringing back masalas, shirts, and hoodies from India to sell here at a higher price. Why am I sharing this? I don’t know, these are just my thoughts. It was your choice to read them.

These few months have been a roller coaster of emotions, making me appreciate the small acts of kindness from others, like making food when I’m sick or sitting silently with me during tough times. I’ve had to maintain long-distance friendships and deal with major FOMO over the simplest things—close friends’ weddings, New Year’s celebrations, friends’ trips, family gatherings, and teasing my sister (though I still do that over the phone).

Many things my parents did started making sense once I began living on my own. One of the most significant mantras that made life easier, which I learned in the first week here and still hold onto, is “Pathukalam” (which roughly translates to “Let’s see whatever it is”).
